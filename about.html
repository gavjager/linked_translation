What is this Thing Anyways?

It is first and foremost a translation website. It is currently under construction as a final project for my German 472 class at the University of Michigan. If I like how it turns out, I might continue to develop it!

I'm choosing to call it a "linked-dynamic" translation, because it uses HTML and Javascript to link poetry and commentary and make the actual text of the poems dynamic. (There's some CSS to make it pretty, too, or at least marginally better than plaintext).

What's so Interesting About It?

The goal of this project is to explore a new way of translating. Good translation should stand on its own as a work of art. It should awake an echo of the original,1 which is to say that it needs to remain connected in an almost resonant way with the source work, but also function on its own terms in the target language. Translation cannot ignore its role as commentary on the original work and on the process of translation itself. Like all art, it thrives on the creation and presentation of context, in that context gives it meaning, with each work then becoming part of the context. Translating, then, relies on an economy of language and information: too little, and the work becomes either cryptic – an exercise in "translation-ese" – or disconnected from the original; too much, and the translation is bogged down, broken up, and becomes merely commentary and disassembly of the original. One of the main difficulties in translation is the struggle to find and maintain a consistent approach, one that respects the economy of language and uses it to create and connect.

The nature of language is such that no word in one language is totally equal to one in another.2 (Take a closer look at this quote). There are many different ways to translate, and the resulting translations will have different syntax, semantics, and meaning in the target language. The trick is then to choose the translation which will create and connect context – this relates directly to Schleiermacher's concept of whether to "move the reader" or "move the writer,"3 but is not necessarily bound to either option. With the dynamic translation techniques that this project is trying to develop, these differences between translations are highlighted, the ability to give context to the reader is increased, and everything is incorporated into a single work which can still stand alone as a work of art.

The danger in all of this is still that the translation could be bogged down by an overabundance of information. Therefore, it is vital that we take advantage of how the work and its context can be presented, and the ways in which language can be used that were not previously possible. It is with this in mind that I decided to keep the dynamic links initially hidden in the poems and give the reader the ability to hide the commentary. The work needs to stand on its own, and the reader needs to have some control over the information which is presented to them.

With the internet, we have a new medium and new rules. We have, in effect, a new economy of language and information, one that lends itself well to the process and product of translation.
